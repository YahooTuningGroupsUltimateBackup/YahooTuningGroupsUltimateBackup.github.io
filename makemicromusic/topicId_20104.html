Gendhing Canright is the first piece with bi-level patterns.
The advantage is in having patterns with more than two durations and greater over-all flexibility.
actually no bars repeat but the first one even though a particulars layer might. more often the 108 would be subdivided
and an overall pattern would be augmented or diminished or retrograded.

It is a funny kind of counterpoint. Since nothing overlaps two or more lines will merge into a single one. Kind of monophonic counterpoint.

I am working on a piece where one of these patterns is used to determine return of themes/textures but the durations of these sections is relatively free.
Kind of building a new piece on the ruins of an old.
Outside of that, i will wait to have enough new ideas before i play with this more.

This is all very good. These scales all seem to follow a kind of hazy/vague mood...but they are all quite "in mood" and beautifully consonant in addition.
Even the 9-note scales sound "stressed" but also in a way where you can predict how stressed the next note will be thus giving it a consonant-like sense of predictability I have found fairly rare in many types of micro-tonal scales including ET scales.
-------------------------------------

What I don't get is, if the horogram keeps circling outward making the lines shorter...how do you get an "infinite" horogram using the golden ratio?

For the record, I (at least) understand the golden ratio is divides a line so one side is always twice as large as the other...but beyond that the use of the ratio for these scale escapes me.

-Michael
