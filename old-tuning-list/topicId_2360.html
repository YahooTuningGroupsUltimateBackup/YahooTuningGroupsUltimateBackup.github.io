Hi Graham (and tuning list at large),

Thanks for your exposition on the use of matrices to represent intervals and
scales (Digest 1166 Topic 2, Subject: Matrices for beginners). Tuning
systems are sometimes hard to visualize, so I always appreciate various ways
of representing them. (I've even resorted to gluing toothpicks together to
help conceptualize 5-limit just intonation. People now think I'm a "psycho"!)

At 04:15 PM 8/27/97 -0700, you wrote
>...
>A C major scale (not the only one) can be defined using t, s and p
>as follows:
>
> C D E F G A B C
> t+p t s t+p t p+t s
>
>This can be written using matrices:

Okay, just to prove I was following you, here's a correction:

You wrote You meant
---------------- ----------------
(C) (5 3 2) (C) (5 2 3)
(B) (5 3 1) (B) (5 1 3)
(A) (4 2 1) (A) (4 1 2)
(G) (3 2 1)(t) (G) (3 1 2)(t)
(F) = (2 1 1)(s) (F) = (2 1 1)(s)
(E) (2 0 1)(p) (E) (2 0 1)(p)
(D) (1 0 1) (D) (1 0 1)
(C) (0 0 0) (C) (0 0 0)

The s and p got confused on the second (top) half of the scale (G,A,B,C).
You wanted to end up with 5 minor tones (t=10/9), 2 diatonic semitones
(s=16/15), and 3 syntonic commas (p=81/80).

By the way, do you know a good way to visualize 4 dimensions, for things
like limit-7 just intonation? (My glued toothpicks only work for 3 dimensions.)

Thanks again,

--Mark
