ELEMENTS - Synaesthesia: Physically, a sensation felt in one part of the body when another part is stimulated. Psychologically, a process in which one type of stimulus produces a secondary, subjective sensation, as when a specific color evokes a specific smell sensation. Better put, it is the experiencing of sensation in one sense modality (eg, visual images) when the actual stimulus is in another modality (eg, sounds). People who report such experiences are called "synaesthetes". The Russian composer Scriabin is reported to have experienced colors when he heard music. Thus, he experienced "chromesthesia," the most common form of syaesthesia.

THE LISTENING ROOM - In today's accelerated world, it's hard to get away from it all to indulge yourself in luxury. We forget that by simply listening to the right music we can relax, revive and empower our well being. Creating your own personal listening room is a celebration of the whole self. It can be a reward to your mind, body and spirit. It is one place you can go to, again and again, to travel to new worlds and attain peace of mind. The purpose of creating the listening room is to have a special place to relax and listen as a ritual of calm and a dawning of clarity. It is your sacred place to unwind, luxuriate, and heal. Listening is both a fine art and a magical act, a ritual connecting you to the power of your sonic affinities. It is an essential element of life that promises enduring rewards. To relax yourself with the auroma of enchanting sounds is to commune with your self. In a sanctum of sound, we can breath freely, be delighted, and find deep enjoyment.

So dim the lights, burn candles and incense, spray aromatic oils, and create your own private retreat where you can enjoy tasty morsels, drink wine, smoke cigars, read books, write letters, paint pictures, smell fragrances, or do nothing but listen. Feel how all of your senses are connected - what you see, taste and hear is all part of your musical gestalt here in the listening room. The entire music "industry" can be interpreted as the manufacture of supernormal stimuli. Fashion, band names, and album art enlarge the artists ego, music video brightens their star, concert staging brings mythological stature, costumes project vigor and advertise status, disc element gains legitimacy when the audience sees a human manipulating the soundscape live. In the process the perception of "DJ" becomes expanded/redefined. This raising of the bar would go a long way towards the day when the obsolete term "DJ" is replaced with a more suitable title that genuinely reflects the diverse roles and skills involved in presenting music in the modern world. Today anyone, from those who can beatmix two records to those who press buttons and speak the time and temperature call themselves a "DJ". The term as a whole has become all too generic, limiting and misunderstood. We need a new way to classify and recognize those with skills that go beyond the general dictionary definition.

We need to have a clear understanding that a DJ and those artistic professionals who embody part mixer/remixer, part engineer, part sound manipulator, part musician, part producer, part programmer are as different as night and day. Our pick? "Mixologist".
