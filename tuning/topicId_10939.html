Okay, my reply is partially based on information at the following websites:

http://www.grandrapids.com/x/WoodstockChimes/9y.htm
http://www.egroups.com/message/tuning/9609
http://nowitzky.hypermart.net/justint/nnn.htm

The notes are (from largest to smallest size, so from lowest to highest
pitch):

3/2 D
8/5 Eb
5/3 E
16/9 F
1/1 G
9/8 A
6/5 Bb
5/4 B
4/3 C
3/2 D

From your set of 10 tubes you could create these sets of 6 tubes each (as
Lydia stated at http://www.egroups.com/message/tuning/9609):

Olympus: D Eb G A Bb D
Partch: D E G A B D
Lun: D F G A C D

Each 6-tube set alone produces a pentatonic scale. The following
combinations (from "favorite combinations" suggested at
http://www.grandrapids.com/x/WoodstockChimes/9y.htm) produce diatonic scales:

Olympus + Lun: D Eb F G A Bb C D (G Æolian)
Partch + Lun: D E F G A B C D (G Mixolydian)

Maybe it's the combination of Olympus with Partch that sounds odd to you.
Or maybe it's just too many notes to combine all three sets. Maybe it's
too windy outside. :)

It might (or might not) help to visualize the tunings in two dimensions.
In the following diagrams,
- Perfect Fifth intervals are horizontal
- Major Third intervals are vertical
- Nowitzkian Note Names (http://nowitzky.hypermart.net/justint/nnn.htm) are
used

All 9 notes (not 10 notes, 'cuz 2 tubes produce 5C):
4E 4B
5F 5C 5G 5D 5A
6Eb 6Bb

6Eb 6Bb

Partch:
4E 4B

Hi, Bekah,

Mark Nowitzky's discussion in his previous email is a good description of the

10 tubes of the Woodstock chimes set.

I started my Woodstock gamelan with one of the 10-chime sets. I spoke to the
people from Woodstock Percussion when I bought it (they were a smaller
company then, and it was at a crafts fair). The 10-chime set came on a
holder which held 6 chimes at a time, and they were intended to be in the
same 6-tone sets as the windchimes they produce in their factory (they
only had three pentatonic scales way back then). The lowest and highest
tones were 3/2 (their more current literature shows it as 1/1 in the key of
D, instead of 3/2 in the key of G). All three pentatonic scales used
3/2, 1/1, 9/8 and 3/2 (D, G, A, D). The other pairs of tones were swapped
around on the modular holder.

I got another holder so I could have all the tones available all the time.
Then I had 2 more empty spaces in the second holder, so I asked them to
make some custom tubes to fill those spaces, and the Woodstock gamelan
kept growing from there.

Anyway, I hope that explains why they have such precision just intonation,
but some of the relactionships can be unexpected when you combine the
3 pentatonic scales together -- they didn't design it to be a 10-tone
set.
