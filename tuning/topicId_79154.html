Last sentence sounds to me like Chinese, from the expression it can be something like "OK, you did well!". But...

Rhythm is too perfect to be pure folk musicians, and they play too mechanical, without expression, like Westerners playing just some notes from the score, one by one, not music. Also intonation of singers is not in tune with alternate tuning of instruments, and way of singing, too. Also timbre of voices is not Chinese, it sounds more like educated Western classical opera voices.

So my guess is it is some camp, summer class or so, of Chinese music for foreigners, and they show what they have learned. Still long way to sound really like Chinese music, it seems to me too Western, too academic. But of course I can be totally wrong.

It seems to contain the notes F-C-G-D-A and the tonic seems to be D

This suggests that it is the scale which I have coded as:

4/0/4

info here:

http://www.lucytune.com/scales/

which is the pentatonic known by various names including:

Blues Pentatonic

Pyongjo Kyemyonjo (Korea)

Minyo (Japan)

or Japanese Hard Descending.

It uses five contiguous notes on the chain of fourths and fifths,

in C it contains the notes

C Eb F G Bb

The origins are prolly revealed by the speech at the end, which tells us the language used between the musicians.

I'm not sure what the tuning is, as it moves around, yet on Melodyne it shows as fairly close to 12edo.

Thank you to those who commented. I recorded it in KuaiZaiTing park
(快哉亭公园) a few hours before my original message. So it's authentically
Chinese but maybe not folk music. At least some of what they do is
Maoist and I'm guessing it's all songs they learned at school.

It's interesting that we had a guess for Vietnam. It does sound
south-east Asian to me. We're nearer to Korea than Vietnam so it
shows there's some continuity on the region.

The plucked string instrument is de-fretted. From looking at his
fingers he seems to be going for Pythagorean, or an exaggeration of it
relative to 12-equal. But every now and then it looks like he fingers
a quartertone. The open strings are tuned to match the erhus and he's
still adjusting it at the start of the piece you heard. I think
there's a high note at the start that's wrong.

I've got more if anybody's interested, and some pictures.
