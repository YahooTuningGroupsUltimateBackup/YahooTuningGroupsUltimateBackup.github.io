Someone on the list once made a remark to the effect of, if Schoenberg
really wanted to avoid consonance, he would have been better off in
something like 13TET. Of course 13TET still has an octave in it, which is
highly consonant. I wondered, somewhat perversely, what would happen if I
tried to construct a scale in which dissonance, that is, the average
dissonance of each interval, is maximized. Sort of an anti-JI.

I'm afraid I haven't followed all of the noise and fury, Plomp and
circumstance of the discussion of dissonance (or "complexity") calculations
so ably discussed by other contributors. Nevertheless, I calculated my own
table of dissonance based on ratio complexity to the 13-limit, and a curve
following the critical band dissonance curve in between those ratios. Now
even I don't think that it's the absolute best approximation of dissonance
there is, but it will do for now. Then I tried step sizes from 10 to 300
cents and intervals based on those steps from one step to 2 octaves. (Since
most of them don't divide evenly into an octave, I could go on
indefinitely. I chose 2 octaves just because most adjacent harmonic
intervals in music are within that range.) I calculated the average
dissonance of the intervals in that scale and sorted the results.

Of course I would have gotten different results with different dissonance
curves. I encourage others to try.

And the most dissonant award goes to: 257 cents.
In the < 100 cents category, it goes to 93 cents.
By the way, the least dissonant is nearly a tie between 200 and 240 cents
(6TET and 5TET).
In the < 100 cents category, most consonant is 63 cents (about Carlos beta).
