If you take the fifteen 7-limit intervals 25/24, 28/27, 36/35, 49/48,
50/49, 64/63, 81/80, 126/125, 245/243, 1029/1024, 1728/1715,
2401/2400, 3136/3125, 4375/4374, 6144/6125 from the list
http://www.kees.cc/tuning/s2357.html which Paul used, and take the
15 choose 4 = 1365 four-element subsets, you will find that 642 of
them give unimodular matricies, and hence give us notations. I
uploaded all of these. The first list is a list of the four intervals
used, and the second a list of the columns of the inverse matrix. If
a number +-n appears on the list, then +-hn, the column vector
consisting of n, round(n log_2(3)), round(n log_2(5)),
round(n log_2(7)), is meant. If there is just a "v", it means it is
not rounded off in this way, and the entire matrix is given in a
compact form as a list of lists; the lists are horizontal but
represent column vectors.

There are vast numbers of blocks and tempered blocks which could be
extracted from all of this, so it would be helpful to know what sort
of thing people are looking for.

I took out some of the rank 2 details. The formula I have for scalar badness in the paper has the geometric properties I was looking for then.

Equation 79 shows the badness in terms of "badness vectors". Maybe the custom notation obscures that, but each vector is the original mapping minus the weighted mean. So it's a weighted generalization of the covariance matrix -- which I do mention. The parameterized badness has the same properties because it's a linear combination of scalar complexity and badness (give or take some squaring).

Under "Wedgie Complexity" I give the reference for the volume of a matrix. I don't go into the geometric details but you can extrapolate them from that observation.

Under "Wedgie Error" I explain that it isn't as easy to calculate the error from unison vectors as I originally claimed :(

Singular value decompositions are certainly interesting, and I originally used them in my first TOP-RMS error proof, but I ended up not needing them. One of the matrices you get from an SVD seems to uniquely identify the temperament class.

The connection between scalar complexity and the number of notes in a periodicity block is the main thing I forgot to add when I declared the paper near-enough finished. I toyed with adding it anyway but decided not to.
